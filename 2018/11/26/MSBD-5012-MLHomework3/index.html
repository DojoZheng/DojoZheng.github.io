a.2 What is the logit $z=u_{21}w_{1}^{(3)}+u_{22}w_2^{(3)}$ calculated at the output unit?

$z=u_{21}w_{1}^{(3)}+u_{22}w_2^{(3)}=1 \times 1 + 1 \times 1 = 2$

a.3 What is the probability distribution of the output unit?

$P(y | \mathbf{x})=\sigma((2y-1)z)=\sigma(4y-2)$

(b) Answer:

b.1 What is the error $\frac{\partial L}{\partial z}$ for the output unit?

$\frac{\partial L}{\partial z}=\delta_z=\sigma(z)-y=0.88$

b2. What are the errors for the hidden units?

For each hidden unit j: $\delta_j=\frac{\partial u_j}{\partial z_j}\sum_k{W_{kj}\delta_k}$
$\Rightarrow$
For the second hidden layer:
$\delta_{21}=\frac{\partial{u_{21}}}{\partial{z_{21}}} \times (1\times0.88)=0.88$
$\delta_{22}=\frac{\partial{u_{22}}}{\partial{z_{22}}} \times (1\times0.88)=0.88$

For the first hidden layer:
$\delta_{11}=\frac{\partial{u_{11}}}{\partial{z_{11}}} \times (-0.88-0.88)=0$
$\delta_{12}=\frac{\partial{u_{12}}}{\partial{z_{12}}} \times (0.88+0.88)=1.76$

b.3 What are $\frac{\partial L}{\partial w_{22}^{(2)}}$ and $\frac{\partial L}{\partial w_{22}^{(1)}}$?

For a weight $W_{ji}$ in a hidden layer: $\frac{\partial L}{\partial W_{ji}}=u_i \delta_j$
$\Rightarrow$
$\frac{\partial L}{\partial w_{22}^{(2)}}=u_{12} \delta_{22}=1 \times 0.88=0.88$
$\frac{\partial L}{\partial w_{22}^{(1)}}=x_2 \delta_{12}=2 \times 1.76=3.52$

Answer:

Sigmoid units are not recommended for hidden units because they saturates (becomes flat, has very small derivative) across most of their domains.
However, they are fine as output units because the negative log-likelihood in the cost function helps to avoid the problem:

Saturation occurs only when the model already has the right answer: When y=1 and z is very positive, or y=0 and z is very negative.

Question 3

What is dropout used for in deep learning? How does it work? Why does it work? Answer briefly.

What is dropout used for in deep learning?

It is a regularization method to reduce test error and avoid overfitting for deep learning.

How does it work?

  • It is to be used with minibatch-based algorithms such as SGD.
  • For each minibatch,
    • randomly sample values for mask variables
    • carry out one step of gradient descent.
  • There is only one network, no subnetworks. At each step, training is conducted in a part of the network (a subnetwork).

Why does it work?

It reduces overfitting by preventing complex co-adaptations of parameters on training data.

Question 4

The following picture shows the Adam Algorithm:

Adam optimization algorithm is an extension to Stochastic Gradient Descent. It is roughly a combination of RMSProp and Momentum, with bias correction.

Adam is different to classical stochastic gradient descent. Stochastic gradient descent maintains a single learning rate for all weight updates and the learning rate does not change during training. However, Adam computes individual adaptive learning rates for different parameters from estimates of first and second moments of the gradients.

Meanwhile, it realizes the benefits of both AdaGrad and RMSProp. Instead of adapting the parameter learning rates based on the average first moment (the mean) as in RMSProp, Adam also makes use of the average of the second moments of the gradients (the uncentered variance).

Specifically, the algorithm calculates an exponential moving average of the gradient and the squared gradient, and the parameters beta1 and beta2 control the decay rates of these moving averages.

The initial value of the moving averages and beta1 and beta2 values close to 1.0 (recommended) result in a bias of moment estimates towards zero. This bias is overcome by first calculating the biased estimates before then calculating bias-corrected estimates.

(a)

Yes, it is a good idea to apply dropout to a Convolutional Network because dropout is widely used to solve the problems of overfitting in CNN.

We should apply dropout to the Fully Connected Layers in CNN because the parameters density in the Fully Connected Layers is very high which may lead to the problems of overfitting.

(b)

As for RNN, it depends on the situation.

The following pictures show the two dropout technique for RNN.

  • Naive Dropout RNN
    It is not a good idea to apply dropout to recurrent layers (connections between RNN units) because noise will be amplified for long sequences and drown the signal.

    However, we can apply dropout mask at each time step for inputs, outputs and hidden layers. It looks like the connections with different colours in picture (a). The dashed lines represent the connections with no dropout.

  • Variational RNN
    Picture (b) shows the Variational RNN with Bayesian technique. It uses the same dropout mask between recurrent layers (connections between RNN units). Meanwhile, it applies the same dropout mask for all the inputs, outputs and hidden layers, respectively.
